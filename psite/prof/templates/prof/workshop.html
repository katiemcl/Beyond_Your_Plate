The complexity of this process that we discovered through these conversations guided us conclude that there is no simple solution to providing affordable, sustainable options for local restaurants. Instead, we pulled inspiration from existing theories to extend beyond solutions and encourage ruminating in the complex problem itself.

Our workshop lasted 45 minutes and was broken down into three sections: laying down initial assumptions, storytelling the preparation process, and a meal with discussion.

pre-workshop

The event informally started a little over two weeks before the in-person component of the event. Two Mondays before the event, we sent an email to all attendees detailing the process Lynn has to go through to buy ingredients. Because prices are highly variable (especially for fish), she must call all of her vendors every week to compare prices and ensure that she is receiving quality ingredients for an affordable price.

We also sent an email for each of the Tuesdays before, the night before, and the morning of our event to shine a light on the amount of time and labor that goes into preparing a few dishes.

part 1: naming assumptions

Our opening exercise guided participants to describe their initial assumptions about food sourcing. We asked participants to jot their answers on their placemat. This placement consisted of the menu items we were serving at the end of the workshop as well as empty boxes to be filled with reflection notes. The intention of this initial exercise was to orient our participants and make their own assumptions salient before engaging with the stories.

Placemat with menu of items served at the event. Designed by Raya.

part 2: storytelling

The next aspect of our workshop was storytelling the laborious preparation process. This segment pulled inspiration from the concept of “matters of concern”, which are conditions that rely on subjective experiences and political conditions. The goal in this segment is not to ideate solutions, but to engage with the issues themselves. In our workshop’s case, the matter of concern was the complex relationship between capitalism and sustainability from a small, family-owned restaurant’s perspective. We offer no immediate solution, but instead, leave it open-ended for discussion. With her permission, we wove in recorded clips of our conversation from Lynn. With an animation of the preparation process playing in the background, these elements helped visceralize the experience and stories for the event participants. While the stories were spoken, we asked participants to draw on the bottom of their glass plates with pieces of the story that resonated with them or how they felt during this process.

A frame of the animation that visualizes the preparation of salmon teriyaki. Made by Ashley.

part 3: eating and reflecting

We ended our workshop over a meal from the restaurant itself and a space to discuss our reflections. Eating off of the decorated glass plate, participants could see through to the placemat. This interaction encourages participants to think about what happens beyond their plate and reflect how their new learnings sit with their initial assumptions.

A sentiment that consistently arose during the discussion was the shocking realization of how laborious and time-consuming food preparation is. In relation to sustainability, various points were brought up from sympathy to suggestions such as raising prices to help afford sustainable ingredients. Clarifying questions about ingredient sourcing and what restaurants did with food waste came up. Aside from referencing our interview with Vincent about food waste, we weren’t able to answer these questions, highlighting the lack of transparency in ingredient sourcing.
